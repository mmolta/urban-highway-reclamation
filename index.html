Urban Renewal

initiaves in the 1950's and 1960's promised to raise the standard of living and increase economic prosperity by connecting suburbs to cities. In reality they were just a way to destroy black neighborhoods and allow white flight to happen. They've divided cities, created unsafe neighborhoods and contributed to the slow decay of urban centers.

In the past 30 years, interest in removing urban freeawys has grown as more and more successful projects show the clear social, environmental and economic benefits. Creating space for communities.

16 completed projects nationwide
8.7 miles of freeway removed from cities
$2.7 billion spent on freeway removal
$14.2 billion in estimated economic impact

While the numbers speak for themselves, the impact of urban highway reclamation projects go far beyond numbers. In creating new community spaces, cities are able to develop in previously impossible ways. Talk about the opportunity cost of NOT removing them. Most of these highways were old and in need of major repairs. For example, the $45 million Park East Freeway removal in Milwaukee represented "a big savings over the $100 million that it would have cost to rebuild the freeway, which was 30 years old and in need of reconstruction." (source.) Same deal for Rochester's Inner Loop: "Maintenance of this portion of the Inner Loop would exceed the cost of filling the loop in and creating an at-grade street, while providing none of the benefits" (source.)

Bring up the concerns of gentrification. Low income neighborhoods were destroyed to build the freeways and the surrounding area became worthless land. Land value goes up once the freeway is removed and it's crucial for these projects to not double down on mistakes of the past and prioritize people alrady living there. It would be a disaster to remove the freeway and price out locals.
