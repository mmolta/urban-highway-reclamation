Urban Highway Reclamation

"The removal of an urban highway creates the opportunity to reclaim part of its former right-of-way for development, which boosts a city's tax base, provides access to jobs, and increases household wealth along the corridor" - Congress for New Urbanism

City planning

initiatives in the mid 20th century paved highways through American cities to facilitate connection to developing suburbs. The movement became known as "urban renewal" and, with the justification of reducing "urban blight", low-income and predominantly black neighborhoods were destroyed to make space for highways. Construction tore up communities, divided cities, and reduced the air quality of the surrounding areas. The priorities of urban spaces began to change and the people living in them became secondary to the cars moving throughout them.

As the highways built during "urban renewal" fall into disrepair, there are opportunities to re-imagine healthier, better connected cities where the needs of people living within them matter more than the movement of goods and people through them.

Cities must either pay to repair these highways or pay to remove them, and it often costs less to tear down than to repair. For example, the $45 million Park East Freeway removal in Milwaukee cost less than half of the $100 million that it would have cost to rebuild the freeway . Furthermore, the fear of increased congestion caused by removing urban freeways has been repeatedly proven to be misplaced, with the earthquake-induced removal of the Embarcadero Freeway serving as a case study. As the dataset of successful projects grows, the evidence in favor of removing urban highways becomes increasingly difficult to ignore:

It is difficult to quantify the impact of urban highway removal due to the lack of available data and the broad range of qualitative benefits. Dollars returned on investment do not accurately capture the social benefits including quality of life improvements. Many projects do not report the environmental impact although the ones that do show significant improvements. (link here).

The logistics and scope of considerations when removing urban highways are vast. The Congress for New Urbanism and Transportation for America have outlined principles that focus on policy action as well as community, environmental and economic guidelines for effective highway removal.

The following section of this page will provide a high-level overview of completed, developing and actively planned urban highway removal projects. The list is not comprehensive because it imposes constraints on project type, as well as proposal type. For example, highway caps are not considered and neither are grassroots campaigns that have not resulted in official adoption of a plan.

Glossary

  • Induced Demand
    The phenomenon of adding more lanes to a freeway and ending up with more traffic. This happens because more lanes invites more cars which creates more traffic.
  • Viaduct
    An elevated section of highway.
  • Urban Renewal
    City planning movement in the 1950's and 1960's, championed by Robert Moses, that pushed for the construction of highways through city centers. The idea was to facilitate movement through cities into suburbs and other cities and also to reduce traffic. Undoing the harms caused by urban renewal is the focus of this website.
