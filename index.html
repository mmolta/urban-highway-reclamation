Urban Highway Reclamation

"The removal of an urban highway creates the opportunity to reclaim part of its former right-of-way for development, which boosts a city's tax base, provides access to jobs, and increases household wealth along the corridor"

- Congress for New Urbanism

The problem

City planning initiatives in the mid 20th century paved highways through American cities to facilitate connection to developing suburbs. The movement became known as "urban renewal" and, with the justification of reducing "urban blight", low-income and predominantly Black neighborhoods were destroyed to make space for highways. Construction tore up communities, divided cities, and reduced the air quality of the surrounding areas.

The priorities of urban spaces began to change and the people living in cities became secondary to the cars moving throughout them. As the highways built during "urban renewal" fall into disrepair, citizens and elected officials have new opportunities to redistribute space dominated by vehicles and transform what it means to live and move in a city.

The opportunities

Managing traffic was a primary argument in favor of building, expanding, and maintaining urban highways but history has shown the opposite to be true. The earthquake-induced removal of the Embarcadero Freeway and the collapse of the Westside Freeway are case studies on the inefficacy of urban highways. These forced removals inpsired other cities to consider alternatives to maintaining highways.

There is no cost-free option to dealing with failing infrastructure, and therein lies the opportunity to undo mistakes of the past. For example, the $45 million Park East Freeway removal in Milwaukee cost less than half of the $100 million that it would have cost to rebuild the freeway . The project revitalized downtown Milwaukee, created three new neighborhoods, and generated over a billion dollars in investment all while costing $55 million less than keeping the existing structure that provided none of the benefits.

The benefits

It is important to contextualize the numbers associated with urban highway removal. Private investment statistics tell a positive yet incomplete story. Ultimately, the effects of urban highway removal are more qualitative than quantitative. The quality of life benefits are continuous and generational. As communities rebuild themselves, the city as a whole becomes stronger and more attractive. New connections and patterns of social and cultural exchange take time to develop and establish themselves. Physical and mental health benefits from improved air quality take time to manifest.

The overall benefit of removing urban highways is creating safe, clean, accessible space in which people can thrive. Redistributing the space dominated by automobiles allows citizens to reclaim their space and It is therefore crucial to be conscious of equity when planning beyond urban highways. The Congress for New Urbanism and Transportation for America have outlined principles that focus on policy action as well as community, environmental and economic guidelines for effective highway removal.

Glossary

  • Induced Demand
    The phenomenon of adding more lanes to a freeway and ending up with more traffic. This happens because more lanes invites more cars which creates more traffic.
  • Viaduct
    An elevated section of highway.
  • Spur
    A short road forming a branch from a larger road. In the context of this project, spurs are often incomplete sections of highways that were meant to circle cities.
  • Urban Renewal
    City planning movement in the 1950's and 1960's, championed by Robert Moses, that pushed for the construction of highways through city centers. The idea was to facilitate movement through cities into suburbs and other cities and also to reduce traffic. Undoing the harms caused by urban renewal is the focus of this website.
  • Urban Blight
    At surface level, urban blight refers to rundown parts of cities. The reality of the phrase exposes deep, structural and institutional inequalities in America that have resulted in a strong correlation between areas of urban blight and predominantly Black neighborhoods.
  • Corridor
    A section of roadway, i.e. a stretch between exits on a highway or some other geographic bounding.

Disclaimer

Last updated on December 15th, 2022

Projects were from the extent of my research, some may be missing and some were omitted intentionally. Viewing CNU's list of highways to boulevard fact sheet shows a greater number of completed projects than on this website. I found that some were still in development and needed to be reclassified, some of the planned projects seem to have been outright rejected by their local government and others are too early in the planning/activism stage. I also did not include highway capping projects because, while they do reconnect neighborhoods in the short term, they do not remove the problem and seem to create a similar set of maintenance issues. How long can they stay safe, how will construction on top of them, root erosion and other factors impact the safety of drivers underneath them?

The point of this website is to show projects that have happened or will certainly happen. I also wanted to focus on projects that met sustainable criteria in every sense of the word. The maintenance concerns associated with capping projects and the lack of time available to fully research their long term effects led me to exclude them.

Figures were calculations based on reported numbers in articles and on official websites, everything on this page is an estimate. The point of this project is to give a general overview on the status of Urban Highway Reclamation and make a case that it is a strong area for social, environmental, and economic development.

This page was built in two weeks and in order to prioritize research and quality of content, there are some sacrifices on technical aspects that had to be made. For one, point clustering on the map would be nice to have but is not included. Map filters did not make the cut either.
