Urban Highway Reclamation

"The removal of an urban highway creates the opportunity to reclaim part of its former right-of-way for development, which boosts a city's tax base, provides access to jobs, and increases household wealth along the corridor" - Congress for New Urbanism

Urban Renewal

initiaves in the mid 20th century paved highways through American cities to facilitate connection to developing suburbs. Under the name of "reducing urban blight", low-income and predominantly black neighborhoods were destroyed to make space for the new highways. Construction tore up communities, divided cities, and reduced the air quality of the surrounding areas. The priorities of cities began to change as the people living in them became second class to the cars moving throughout them. As the highways from urban renewal efforts begin to decay, there are new opportunities to re-imagine cities as safe, healthy, productive spaces for people to coexist.

There is optimism for change because there is no cost-free solution to dealing with urban highways. Cities must either pay to repair them or pay to remove them, and in many cases it costs less to tear down than to repair. For example, the $45 million Park East Freeway removal in Milwaukee represented " a big savings over the $100 million that it would have cost to rebuild the freeway ". By contrast, " 2008's Katy Freeway expansion took 3 years to complete and cost $2.8 billion. ". Not only can repairing or expanding freeways be more expensive, but decades of urban planning research have shown, through adding lanes to roads often increases travels time due to a induced demand .

Disclaimer

Last updated on December 15th, 2022

Projects were from the extent of my research, some may be missing. Figures were calculations based on reported numbers in articles and on official websites, everything on this page is an estimate. The point of this project is to give a general overview on the status of Urban Highway Reclamation and make a case that it is a strong area for social and economic development.

For the sake of time, capping projects were not considred. I chose to constrain the scope of this website to projects that removed parts or entire freeways from an urban environment. Freeway caps are a popular solution that can help reconnect divided neighborhoods. An evolution of this website would addd them to the map.

This page was built in two weeks and in order to prioritize research and quality of content, there are some sacrifices on technical aspects that had to be made. For one, point clustering on the map would be nice to have but is not included.

Many proposed or discussed projects were not chosen for this website because the conversations are too early stage, too theoretical, or seem to have been abandoned. Projects like I-275 in Tampa, the Claiborne Expressway (etc., add links). They may eventually become useful but I want to create this website as a repository of projects that are completed, close to completed, or seriosly in consideration. Despite a lot of great work and community, grassroots organizations, a fair number of projects like the Tampa and other ones don't make the cut for this page because there is simply too much uncertainty around their future.
